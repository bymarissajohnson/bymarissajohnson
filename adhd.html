My story

I thought I got rid of my ADHD.
Turns out, I just had structure.

I was diagnosed at 6 and medicated young. The meds were hard on me, so my mom and my 5th grade teacher — Ms. Howard — helped me build coping mechanisms so I could get off them. By high school I was unmedicated and thriving academically. I went through high school, college, and the Navy without medication and without looking back. I genuinely thought I had outgrown it.

But when I left the Navy, I lost the structure that had been holding everything together without me even knowing it. The transition to civilian life turned my world upside down. At 29, I was rediagnosed — not because I had gotten worse, but because for the first time in my adult life, no one was building the container for me. I had to build it myself.

I went back on medication, but I always knew I'd want to come off them again one day. So over the last 5 years, I built these tools — starting in my notes app, getting frustrated with that, and finally putting everything in one place that's actually easy to use throughout the day, the week, and the month. This is that place.

Tool 01

The Daily Planner

Marissa's story

The military gave me structure I didn't know I needed. Everything had a time, a place, a procedure. And I thrived. But the moment that external structure was gone — law school, civilian life, building a brand — I fell apart in ways I couldn't explain at the time.

I'd write 47 tasks on a sticky note and freeze. Too many decisions, too much on one page. I needed something that made the choice for me — something that said "start here" and actually meant it. Something that felt forgiving, not punishing, when the day didn't go exactly as planned.

"Top 3. That's it. Everything else is a bonus. Your brain can hold three things — and three things done is a genuinely successful day."

This planner is built around that principle. Priority-based, energy-aware, dopamine-friendly. It doesn't ask you to be perfect. It just asks you to start.

Tool 02

Weekly Brain Dump

Marissa's story

I hit a point where my brain felt so full that I couldn't even start anything. I would sit down to plan my day and just stare at the page — because everything felt equally urgent and overwhelming at the same time. My to-do list had a to-do list.

I realized I didn't need a better plan. I needed to get everything out of my head first before I could even think clearly. Once I started doing that — just dumping everything with no order, no categories, no pressure — planning became possible. This has been extremely helpful. I genuinely cannot live without my brain dump list.

"I didn't need a better plan. I needed an empty head. The brain dump is what makes everything else work."

Dump everything on Sunday. No categories, no order, no judgment. Then each morning, pull 1–3 things into your daily planner. Check them off here when done. Watch the list shrink all week — that's your dopamine hit.

Tool 04

Focus Timer — Pomodoro Method

Marissa's story

In law school, I physically could not sit and study for hours the way everyone else seemed to. I would either get distracted immediately or hyperfocus on entirely the wrong thing and burn out before I'd even touched what I actually needed. I was failing at the effort before the work even began.

I started using timers as a way to force myself to just begin — telling myself "just 25 minutes, that's it" — and it was the first time studying felt manageable instead of impossible. Not just manageable. Actually doable. I still use it for everything now: content, emails, writing, anything that requires sustained focus. The timer is the signal my brain needs to actually start.

"Just 25 minutes. You don't have to finish — you just have to start. The timer does the rest."

Why it works for ADHD — the science

ADHD brains struggle with time blindness — tasks feel either infinite or completely invisible, which makes starting feel impossible. The Pomodoro Technique (developed by Francesco Cirillo) breaks work into 25-minute focused sprints followed by a 5-minute break. The timer creates a container your brain can actually commit to. The break is the reward. The cycle becomes the system. For ADHD specifically, it bypasses task paralysis by lowering the activation cost — you're not committing to finishing anything. Just to starting for 25 minutes. That distinction is everything.

What actually works

Marissa's real ADHD strategies

Not from a productivity book. From a brain that has had to figure it out across three completely different lives.

📋

The 3-Task Rule

Pick 3 things. That's your whole day. Everything else is bonus. ADHD brains can hold 3 — and 3 things done is a genuinely successful day.

🌀

Brain Dumping

Get everything out of your head before you try to plan. An empty head is the only place a clear plan can actually land. Dump first, organize second.

⏰

Time Blocking

Every task gets a time slot. No slot, no starting. Structure removes the decision so you can create the freedom to actually begin.

👯‍♀️

Body Doubling

Work alongside someone — in person, on a call, or even a study stream. The presence of another person activates focus in a way willpower alone can't.

📅

Sunday Reset

One hour every Sunday. Brain dump, weekly plan, reset your space. Starting the week with intention is the difference between reactive and purposeful living.
